In Under Control, each game cycle begins when new problems threaten production, profit, or the company's image. The player must respond quickly, deciding how to contain the issue while keeping the business moving. These decisions often involve hiding information, cutting corners, or pushing people and resources harder than is safe.

When containment succeeds, the company earns short-term profit and stability, which makes it feel like the right call. Over time, however, these choices quietly increase long-term risk. As profits rise, the problems become larger and more frequent, forcing the player into harsher and more uncomfortable decisions.

The loop reinforces the idea that efficiency and control are rewarded, while accountability is consistently delayed.

Game Systems

The Mechanics of Control

Executive Dashboard / Lobby

This is where the player spends most of their time. It acts as the main hub, showing the overall state of the company, including profit, public perception, worker wellbeing, and environmental damage. New emails, alerts, and reports constantly appear here, creating a sense of urgency. From this screen, players decide which problem to deal with next, reinforcing the feeling that there is never enough time or attention to go around.

Crisis / Encounter Screen

This is where things get tense. Urgent situations play out here, such as a journalist showing up unexpectedly, an inspection warning, or a whistleblower threatening to speak out. These moments are often time-pressured and force players to make fast decisions about what to reveal, hide, or manipulate. Success comes from containing the situation before it spirals into something bigger.

Supply Chain Management Screen

This screen focuses on the long-term side of the job. Players adjust labor costs, materials, factory locations, and production timelines to keep profits high and deadlines met. While these choices feel strategic and efficient, they quietly increase long-term harm and make future problems more severe.

Cover-Up / Stealth Sequences

These are short, hands-on moments where players actively hide evidence. Tasks might include deleting emails, editing worker records, staging factory floors, relocating workers, or rebranding harmful practices. These sequences are chaotic and stressful, highlighting how much work goes into keeping problems out of sight.

Narrative Feedback Layer

The game speaks to the player through a steady stream of news headlines, internal messages, worker complaints, and environmental reports. At first, these updates feel distant and manageable, just background noise to a busy job. Over time, they begin to tell a different story. Small decisions resurface as larger consequences, and problems that once seemed "handled" return in more visible and damaging ways. Without directly blaming the player, the narrative quietly reveals how their choices ripple outward, showing harm that continues to grow even as the company appears to stay under control.
